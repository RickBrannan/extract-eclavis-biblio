1. SUMMARY

The Acts of Thomas narrates the apostle Thomas’s miracles and preaching as he makes his way across India. The narratives are often a simple nature, and are supplemented by extended homilies, prayers, songs, and blessings. The number and extent of these spoken materials has attracted numerous additional materials to be added, of which the Hymn of the Pearl is the best known. The first act. The apostles divide the world amongst themselves, but Thomas refuses to go to India as instructed. Thomas’s Lord, Jesus, sells him to the merchant Abban, who promises to take him to be a craftsman for King Gundaphorus in India. En route, Thomas and Abban participate in a wedding banquet for a king’s daughter, at which Thomas sings a song about the church. Here he is assaulted by a cupbearer who then subsequently perishes for his treatment of the apostle, after which time a Hebrew flute player and some of the guests become convinced to listen to Thomas. Because of this, the king hears of Thomas, and he invites the apostle to pray for the bride-to-be and her groom in the bridal chamber. Jesus assumes the form of Thomas and preaches to the bride and groom; they convert, and do not engage in intercourse during the night. At first the king is angry for the conversion of his daughter to an encratic view of marriage, and he wishes to punish the apostle as a sorcerer, but he has already begun to move further into India. Later, however, numerous converts follow the message of Thomas, and journey to join him. The second act. The merchant Abban and Thomas reach their destination, and Abban delivers Thomas to King Gundaphorus to be his craftsman. The king tasks Thomas with building him a new palace, but Thomas instead distributes the building funds to charity. The king imprisons Thomas and Abban, but when the king’s brother Gad dies, he discovers that Thomas has actually built the king a palace in heaven. Gad is sent back to earth, and discloses the miracle to Gundaphorus, whereupon both of them are converted and celebrate the Eucharist with Thomas and others. The third act. Thomas and his companions discover a young man who has been slain by a wicked serpent. At the insistence of Thomas, the snake reveals his demonic identity. Thomas then requires the serpent to suck his own poison out of the dead youth, which results in the death of the serpent and the resurrection of the boy. The act closes with songs of praise and sermons on the benefits of the encratic life. The fourth act. A donkey approaches Thomas and asks to bear him into the city. At the request of Thomas, the donkey reveals its lineage from Balaam’s ass and the donkey that bore Jesus into Jerusalem. After Thomas rides the beast into the city, it dies, having fulfilled its purpose. The fifth act. In the city, Thomas meets a woman who has been tormented by an amorous demon for five years. Thomas casts out the demon, the woman and others are converted, and the new converts celebrate the Eucharist. The sixth act. Thomas meets a young man whose hands shrivelled up when he took the Eucharist, and learns that this happened because he had murdered a woman who refused to forsake sexual activity. Thomas causes the woman to return from the grave, and she narrates the horrors of hell. Hymnic and sermonic discourses by Thomas follow these narrative events. The seventh act. A military captain named Siphor asks Thomas to heal his wife and daughter, both of whom have been miserably oppressed by demons for three years. Thomas appoints a deacon and offers public prayers, and then leaves with Siphor. The eighth act. The animals carrying Thomas and Siphor become too exhausted to pull their carriage, and Thomas enlists the aid of a herd of wild asses. Thomas sends one of the asses into Siphor’s home to exorcise the demons; the women become unresponsive. Thomas begins to carry out a discussion with the demons, one of which had previously been exorcised by Thomas. The ass and Thomas deliver hymns and exhortations, and Thomas in the end raises up the women and sends the asses back outside the city. The ninth act. A noblewoman named Mygdonia becomes convinced by the preaching of Thomas, and refuses to lie with Charisius, her husband. Charisius, concerned that Thomas is a sorcerer who has placed an enchantment over his wife, has the apostle brought before King Misdaeus. Thomas is thrown in prison and sentenced to death. Two manuscripts (Vallicellanus B 35 and British Library Add. 14, 645) insert the Hymn of the Pearl at this point. Charisius returns home to Mygdonia, and discovers that these actions have not cured her. The tenth act. Mygdonia continues to pursue another audience with Thomas, who comes to her and her nurse Narkia in secret; he baptizes them both, and they celebrate the Eucharist. Misdaeus and Charisius continue to threaten Thomas, but nothing changes Mygdonia’s heart. Thomas baptizes and celebrates the Eucharist with Siphor and his household. The eleventh act. Misdaeus tells Tertia, his wife, to visit Mygdonia to convince her to take up her former way of life. Instead, Tertia is converted, and returns to Misdaeus with the desire to convert him to Thomas’s way of life. Misdaeus and Charisius again arrest Thomas. The twelfth act. The king commands his son, Vazan, to manage the soldiers guarding Thomas. Vazan is convinced by Thomas’s message, and seeks to help Thomas escape. Misdaeus, enraged, commands that Thomas be tortured with heated iron plates, but a flood of water erupts to protect Thomas from harm. When the water threatens to inundate the palace, the king begs Thomas to stop the flooding. Thomas is returned to prison, and he offers speeches and prayers of farewell. The thirteenth act and the Martyrdom of Thomas. Vazan asks Thomas to heal his wife, Mnasara, with whom he has lived in chastity. Thomas heals her, and with everyone collected in Vazan’s house, the couple are baptized. Thomas returns to prison, and has one final confrontation with Misdaeus. Misdaeus commands that Thomas be taken outside of the city and put to death. After prayers and speeches (and after appointing Siphor to be a priest and Vazan to be a deacon), Thomas is executed by soldiers with spears, and is buried in the tombs occupied by ancient kings. An apparition of Thomas appears to Siphor and Vazan and encourages them to return to the city. Misdaeus and Charisius continue to afflict their wives, but do not persuade them to abandon chastity. Later, Misdaeus’ son is afflicted by a demon, and a vision of Thomas tells Misdaeus that he can be cured. Misdaeus employs dust from the place where Thomas’ bones had previously rested as the cure for his son. Misdaeus is convinced, and joins the others who had previously converted.

3.1.7.3 Menologion of Basil II

Preliminary translation: October 6. The struggle of St. Thomas the Apostle. After the ascension of our Lord Jesus Christ, when each of the apostles went to the region which had fallen to them by lot to teach, Saint Thomas reached the region of the Indians, where he preached Christ. But when he had converted the wife of the king of the Indians and his son to the faith of Christ, he was brought to the king, who ordered Thomas to be thrown into prison with the other captives. But having bribed the soldiers with money, the king’s daughter, along with her mother and not a few others, entered the prison and were baptized by him. And after a suitable time priests and deacons were ordained, teaching in the name of Christ. Then they took the saint up to the mountain and killed him with spears.
